I — Introduction
The Morning Manuscript
Algorithmic Selection, Invisible Omission, and the Construction of Visible Reality

Every morning, before most people have had their coffee, an algorithm has already decided what kind of world they live in. This is not a metaphor. It is an operational description of how reality is distributed at planetary scale. I came to understand it not through theory but through practice. For several years I worked on both sides of the feed — managing Meta and Google advertising campaigns, operating TikTok and YouTube content accounts — and in that time I saw what most users are structurally prevented from seeing: visibility is not discovered. It is purchased, engineered, and withheld.

The feed does not surface what exists. It surfaces what has been paid to appear, what behavioral data predicts you will engage with, and what the platform's revenue model rewards. Everything else — the content that was produced, posted, and then quietly made to disappear — is not erased. It is simply rendered invisible. The distinction between these two things is the subject of this paper.

The archival object anchoring this argument is Glimpse [Tetenbaum & Chen, 2011], an artist's book structured around a series of sleeve-cut windows: rectangular apertures that reveal only fragments of cards housed within each sleeve. The reader must perform a physical gesture — pulling the card out through the window — to access its full content. No fragment is complete. Every piece of legible meaning is contingent on an act of retrieval that the book's own architecture both invites and delays. The material logic of Glimpse — partial revelation, designed concealment, meaning contingent on the system's architecture rather than the world's fullness — is the formal argument of this paper made tangible.

The scale at which this logic now operates makes it impossible to treat as a curatorial concern alone. Billions of people receive their daily understanding of the world through TikTok, Instagram, Google Search, and YouTube. Through selection, ranking, and personalization — through the continuous operation of algorithmic systems whose criteria are proprietary, invisible, and unaccountable — they actively determine what becomes legible as "the world." What surfaces is the world. What does not surface does not, for most users, exist as an object of attention, concern, or thought.

This paper argues that algorithmic information feeds function not as windows onto reality but as dynamic archival systems — in #Foucault's sense, the operative law governing what can be seen; in #Derrida's sense, a structure of power masquerading as neutral storage. Like the traditional archive, the feed writes reality through selection and omission. Unlike it, this power operates in real time, at planetary scale, and below the threshold of user awareness. The archive has always been a technology of power. What this paper asks is what happens when that technology becomes invisible infrastructure — and what happens when it begins not merely to curate, but to generate, the content it distributes. The argument proceeds through theoretical framework, historical precedent, three case studies, and a forward-looking analysis of AI-driven feed systems, arriving at a single conclusion: the archive is not becoming more neutral. It is becoming more total.

II — Theoretical Framework
The Law of What Can Be Seen

#Foucault's concept of the archive, as developed in The Archaeology of Knowledge [1969], is not a description of a building or an institution. It is a description of a system. The archive, for Foucault, is "the law of what can be said" — the set of rules governing which statements can appear as legitimate knowledge in a given historical moment. It operates through managed scarcity: what enters the field of discourse is not the natural emergence of truth from a neutral medium, but the product of power acting on language. Crucially, and this is the feature of Foucault's formulation that matters most here, the archive is invisible to those who inhabit it. It cannot be described in its totality from within the historical moment it governs. Its authority depends precisely on this invisibility. The archive does not announce itself as a system of power; it presents itself as the given conditions of intelligibility.

This paper extends Foucault's formulation from statements to images, and from historical discourse to real-time digital infrastructure. The algorithmic feed is the contemporary archive governing what can be seen. It determines, through selection and ranking, which images, which claims, which events, which identities surface as objects of knowledge — and which are compressed into algorithmic irrelevance. Like Foucault's archive, it operates not by prohibiting specific content but by structuring the conditions of visibility. And like Foucault's archive, it is invisible to those who inhabit it: users experience the feed as the world rather than as a system mediating access to the world. The managed scarcity is total precisely because it is imperceptible.

#Derrida's Archive Fever [1996] supplies a second theoretical register. Where Foucault describes the archive as a discursive system, Derrida identifies the archive as a structure of power organized around what he calls the archontic principle: the authority to house records is simultaneously the authority to interpret them. The archons — those who control the archive — determine not only what is preserved but what it means. And the archive's constitutive feature, for Derrida, is absence: the structuring silence, the repression, the document that could in principle have been filed but was not. Applied to the algorithmic feed, Derrida's formulation identifies the platform as the contemporary archon. Google's PageRank algorithm, TikTok's recommendation system, Meta's content graph: these are the mechanisms of archontic authority. They control the ordering of information, and therefore the production of meaning, without public deliberation and without accountability. The black-box design of algorithmic systems — users cannot inspect the criteria by which content is ranked or suppressed — is the contemporary form of archontic opacity.

#Steyerl's "In Defense of the Poor Image" [2009] supplies the third register, and the one that connects the theoretical to the material. Steyerl argues that images exist within a class society of appearances, in which resolution functions as currency. High-resolution images occupy positions of institutional privilege — they are displayed in galleries, licensed by media organizations, reproduced in books. Poor images — compressed, degraded, circulating in low-resolution copies through unofficial channels — are ranked and valued according to their resolution, which is to say according to their relationship to commercial and institutional power. The algorithmic ranking system is the management mechanism of this image class society in its digital form. It determines which content receives the "high-resolution" visibility of the first page — and which is compressed into algorithmic irrelevance, legible in principle but invisible in practice.

The theoretical synthesis this paper proposes is the convergence of all three frameworks: Foucault's archive governs the boundary of the visible; Derrida's archival structure is produced by power rather than truth; Steyerl's image class society installs commercial value as its resolution currency. To analyze algorithmic omission adequately requires all three registers simultaneously, because omission operates at all three levels at once. Some content is suppressed because it violates platform policy; some because it fails engagement metrics; some because it threatens advertiser comfort; some because the design of the system encodes the biases of its makers. These are not the same mechanism, but they share a single structural effect: they determine what the world looks like. This is the archive function of the feed.

III — Historical Touchpoint
The Museum as Structural Precedent

The argument that information systems exercise archival power through selection and omission is not new. It predates the algorithm by centuries. The history of the museum offers the most clarifying precedent — clarifying because it is close enough to be legible and distant enough to be visible as history rather than as the given conditions of knowledge.

In February 1897, a British military expedition entered the Kingdom of Benin in what is today southern Nigeria, burned its capital, deposed its Oba, and looted approximately three thousand objects — bronze plaques, ivory carvings, royal regalia — from the royal palace and its precincts. These objects were dispersed across hundreds of Western museums and private collections, where the majority of them remain. #Hicks, in The Brutish Museums [2020], argues that this dispersal is not a historical event that concluded in 1897. It is an ongoing one. Every day that the museum doors are unlocked and these trophies are displayed to the public, the damage is renewed. The museum's curatorial apparatus — what to display, in what frame, with what interpretive narrative — actively rewrites history rather than preserving it. Looted objects are recast as "universal heritage," as "artistic masterpieces," as "anthropological documents." The act of taking, and the sovereignty of those from whom objects were taken, are structurally omitted from exhibition discourse. The archive does not record their absence. The archive is their absence.

#Hicks proposes, against the traditional "object biography" framework, a methodology he calls necrography: death-histories, or histories of loss. Necrography asks not what objects gained upon entering museum collections, but what their originators were dispossessed of. It reorients the archival inquiry from accumulation to erasure — from what was preserved to what was destroyed, and at whose expense. The central question of necrography is not what the archive shows. It is what the archive makes disappear. This methodological reorientation is the historical mirror of this paper's inquiry into algorithmic omission. The structural parallel between the museum and the feed is not merely analogical; it is constitutive. Both systems exercise archival power through the same sequence of operations: selection, framing, omission, and the naturalization of that sequence as neutral stewardship. The museum naturalizes looting as preservation. The feed naturalizes commercial ranking as relevance. Each morning the museum opens its doors and renews a specific version of history. Each morning the feed refreshes and renews a specific version of the present. The mechanism is the same. The scale is different.

The structural parallel between museum and feed is not merely analogical; it is constitutive. Consider the Elgin Marbles — sculptures from the East pediment of the Parthenon, removed from Athens between 1801 and 1812 under documents of disputed legitimacy, and displayed at the British Museum ever since [Fig. A]. Greece has formally requested their return since 1983. The British Museum's continued refusal to repatriate is not inertia — it is an active archival decision, renewed daily, about whose version of history occupies the first screen.

Artists working in this space have developed counter-archival methods that parallel the necrographic orientation this paper proposes. Brazilian artist #Rennó recycles existing vernacular photographs — identification shots, found portraits, the "lowest categories of the image" — to recover "the little stories of the downtrodden and the vanquished" that successive political regimes have sought to suppress. Her practice of image conservation and recycling, informed by Vilém Flusser's concept of an "ecology of images," treats the archive not as authoritative storage but as material to be redistributed against the grain of institutional power.

#Kaphar operates directly inside the Western archive — appropriating canonical paintings, then cutting away figures, veiling faces, or burying subjects beneath layers of canvas. These are not aesthetic gestures; they are procedural metaphors for erasure, suppression, and omission within historical record-keeping. Kaphar's process makes absence as legible as presence, refusing the closure that would allow historical reckoning to feel complete. The same methodological commitment drives the author's own work [Fig. B] — a book design project that traces the colonial plunder of four cultures (the Tlingit, the Parthenon, China's Yuanmingyuan, Benin City) before turning, in its latter half, toward abstraction and restitution: the "X" on the opening page marking the transformation from a universal "+" to a definitive act of return.

Jeff Wall's Dead Troops Talk [1992] stages a fictional aftermath of a Soviet ambush in Afghanistan — dead soldiers interacting, oblivious to their own deaths. The work refuses documentary photography's claim to neutrality while using its visual language. As a counter-archival gesture, it makes the undocumented visible through deliberate fabrication — a strategy that anticipates questions this paper raises about the generative archive: what happens when the archive begins not merely to suppress the record but to generate one in its place?

IV — Case Studies
Three Sites of Algorithmic Omission

The following three cases demonstrate, across different domains, that algorithmic omission is not incidental but systemic — a consistent expression of the archive power theorized above. Each case illuminates a different face of the same structure. Together, they argue that the feed does not passively distribute a pre-existing world. It produces the world it appears to reflect.

Case Study I — The Visual Archive of Historical Memory

In a 2021 study published in First Monday, #Makhortykh, Urman, and Ulloa [2021] conducted a large-scale algorithmic audit of image search results for the keyword "Holocaust" across six major search engines — Google, Bing, Yahoo, Baidu, Yandex, and DuckDuckGo — deploying two hundred virtual agents to simulate user searches from different geographic and demographic positions. The study documents five forms of systematic malperformance in the image results: misattribution, overrepresentation, trivialization, revisionism, and antisemitism. The aggregate picture is a visual archive of historical memory that is systematically distorted — not randomly, and not by accident.

The most structurally significant finding is not the presence of revisionist or antisemitic content, disturbing as these are. It is the pattern of overrepresentation and absence that organizes the whole. Auschwitz-Birkenau — the largest Nazi extermination site and, after the war, the site of an internationally recognized museum and memorial complex — appears in up to eighty percent of results across certain search engines. The "Holocaust by bullets" in Eastern Europe, in which mobile killing units shot approximately 1.5 million Jewish people in open-air massacres across occupied Soviet territory between 1941 and 1943, is nearly entirely absent from search outputs. This is not a difference in historical significance. The Holocaust by bullets accounts for roughly a quarter of total Nazi genocide victims. Its near-total absence from algorithmic visual results is the product of a specific mechanism: Auschwitz is a major international tourist destination, generating the kind of commercially legible web traffic that algorithmic ranking systems reward. Its visual record is high-resolution, institutionally authorized, widely reproduced, and deeply embedded in the commercial infrastructure of the web. The Eastern European massacre sites are not. This is #Steyerl's image class society operating in the domain of historical memory: visibility is determined by commercial value, not historical significance or ethical responsibility.

The authors make explicit what is only implicit in most discussions of search algorithms: they frame image search not as a library function but as an exhibition one. Unlike text search, which operates as a kind of catalog — directing users to sources where information can be found — image search highlights specific visual aspects of a phenomenon in a way structurally analogous to a museum exhibition. The algorithmic system has replaced the human curator as gatekeeper of historical memory, without inheriting any of the ethical frameworks — informed by scholarship, accountability, and the obligations of representation — that the curatorial tradition attempts to maintain.

Case Study II — The Architecture of Racial Invisibility

In 2010, #Noble conducted a Google image search for the phrase "black girls" while looking for age-appropriate content for her stepdaughter and nieces. The first result was a pornographic website. When she repeated the search two years later, the results were substantially similar. What began as a personal observation became the foundation of a systematic research project, published in 2018 as Algorithms of Oppression [2018], documenting how Google's ranking system consistently associates Black women and girls with pornographic, stereotypical, and dehumanizing content while suppressing accurate, complex, and self-authored representations. Noble names this pattern technological redlining — an extension of the historical practice of racially discriminatory spatial zoning into the architecture of digital information infrastructure.

#Noble's core argument is structural rather than incidental: algorithmic oppression is not a glitch in the system but, rather, is fundamental to the operating system of the web. Three interlocking mechanisms produce this outcome. First, the designers of algorithmic systems carry the biases of the societies that produced them. Second, the economic structure of platforms like Google is advertising-based: the system is commercially incentivized to surface content that generates engagement. Third, the black-box architecture of the ranking system prevents accountability. In #Derridaan terms, Google's PageRank is the contemporary archon: it controls the ordering of information and therefore the production of meaning. It determines what constitutes the primary visual representation of Black women — without public deliberation, without accountability to the communities whose identities it manages, and without the possibility of appeal. The archive's power to produce social reality is not theoretical. It is operational.
Case Study III — Inside the Feed: A First-Person Audit

The two cases above rely on documented research conducted by others. This third case draws on direct practice — and on a methodological approach modeled on the algorithmic auditing methods of Makhortykh et al., adapted to the comparative study of personalized social media feeds rather than search results.

My experience managing advertising campaigns across Meta and Google, and operating content accounts on TikTok and YouTube, provides a practitioner's perspective on the feed that is structurally unavailable to most users. From the back end of the advertising interface, the opacity of the front end resolves into legibility. What appears to users as organic discovery is, from the advertiser's perspective, a bidding environment. Paid promotion is not an add-on to the organic feed. It is interwoven with it, indistinguishable from it at the level of the user interface, and optimized by the same systems that determine organic reach. High-resolution visibility is a commodity with a price. This is #Steyerl's image class society not as theoretical framework but as operational reality.

To observe how the feed constructs divergent versions of reality for different users, I collected first-page feed screenshots from friends and colleagues across different professional fields, interests, demographics, and platform behaviors, at comparable time intervals [Fig. 8–9]. Feeds diverged not only in topic but in affective register, informational density, and implicit worldview. And certain categories of content — politically complex, economically challenging, emotionally demanding — were systematically underrepresented across feeds regardless of individual user interest, consistent with what #Reid terms the "shrinkage funnel" [2024]: a structural narrowing of the information environment that operates prior to and independent of personalization.

This first-person audit reveals the inadequacy of #Pariser's filter bubble model [2011] as a complete account of algorithmic omission. Pariser's argument is that personalization creates ideologically self-reinforcing information environments in which users are progressively insulated from views that challenge their own. This is a real phenomenon. But it is not the deepest problem. The deeper problem is that entire categories of knowledge are compressed out of collective visibility by platform-wide commercial logic that operates prior to any individual's personalization data. The managed scarcity operates at the level of the archive, not the individual user.

Interlude
Cracks in the Archive
Shadowbanning, Misclassification, and the Grammar of Algorithmic Resistance

If the algorithmic feed functions as an archive — systematically governing what can be seen — then its failures of suppression are not mere errors. They are the archive's seams made visible: moments when the system's logic of power becomes legible precisely because it has failed to conceal itself. Two phenomena illuminate this structural dynamic in complementary ways: shadowbanning, in which the system's suppressive function is exposed by its overreach; and algospeak, in which users' resistance to suppression inadvertently confirms the archive's authority.

Shadowbanning — the undisclosed suppression of a user's content visibility without removal or notification — offers one of the most consistently documented instances of algorithmic archive power operating without accountability. The practice has been documented across TikTok, Instagram, Twitter, and YouTube: content exists on the platform, is not removed, is not marked as restricted, but is made algorithmically invisible — its reach reduced to near zero without the creator's knowledge. A 2022 Mozilla Foundation study found that seventy percent of YouTube users flagged for "hate speech" by AI moderators had been misclassified. A 2025 study published in the Journal of Gender Studies documented TikTok's systematic suppression of disabled creators' content, introducing the concept of "algorithmic ableism": the encoding of ableist ideologies into recommendation infrastructure. Black creators on TikTok continued to report content suppression despite the platform's 2020 public apology for what it acknowledged as "systemic problems."

The most extensively documented case of algorithmic misclassification as structural suppression involves Palestinian voices on Meta platforms in the period following October 7, 2023. Between October and November of that year, Human Rights Watch documented over one thousand cases of peaceful pro-Palestinian content being removed or suppressed across Instagram and Facebook. The Palestinian digital rights organization 7amleh documented more than five hundred TikTok videos from Gaza suppressed or removed under "community guidelines" violations. Mondoweiss, a news outlet covering the Israel-Palestine conflict, had its TikTok account permanently banned, reinstated, and banned again in a cycle for which the platform provided no explanation. Access Now's 2024 report characterized the pattern as "rampant, systematic, and global." This is not algorithmic error in the conventional technical sense. It is a form of content governance in which the archive's political alignments become operationally visible through the very act of suppression.

Against this suppressive architecture, users have developed what researchers have termed algospeakALGOSPEAK: coded substitutions to evade algorithmic detection.
"unalive" (suicide) · "watermelon" (Palestinian flag) · "corn" (pornography) — a portmanteau of "algorithm" and "speak," documented by Steen et al. in a 2023 study [Social Media + Society, 2023]. Algospeak refers to the coded substitutions users develop to describe content that would otherwise trigger algorithmic suppression. The practice has historical precedents: the nineteenth-century Russian satirist Mikhail Saltykov-Shchedrin developed "Aesopian language" to circulate political commentary under Tsarist censorship; gay men in early twentieth-century Britain used Polari as a private code of survival. Algospeak is a contemporary form of an ancient grammar — the grammar of speaking within and against a system that determines what can be said.

But the archive learns. Each iteration of algospeak triggers a counter-update in content moderation systems, closing the gap through which marginalized voices briefly passed. The arms race this creates — users encoding, platforms decoding, users re-encoding — does not challenge the archive's authority. It confirms it. The archive's power is demonstrated not by its perfection but by its adaptability: it absorbs resistance into its training data, making the next iteration of suppression more precisely calibrated than the last. This is the archive's most insidious feature. It improves by consuming the evidence of its own failures.

V — The Archive That Writes Itself
AI Agents, Generative Feeds, and the Next Phase of Archival Power

The cases examined above document algorithmic omission as a curation problem: the feed selects from an existing body of content, privileges some, suppresses others. This is already a significant exercise of archival power — power with measurable consequences for historical memory, racial justice, political discourse, and the basic conditions of democratic knowledge. But the current trajectory of AI integration into content systems suggests a qualitative escalation that transforms the problem from one of selection to one of creation. The feed is beginning not merely to curate content but to generate it.

Large language and multimodal models are increasingly deployed not as search tools but as content producers — writing news summaries, generating image captions, synthesizing video. Platforms including YouTube, Instagram, and TikTok are actively integrating generative AI into their recommendation and content-creation pipelines. When the archive begins to generate the documents it archives, #Foucault's "law of what can be said" becomes, in its logical extension, the law of what can exist. The distinction between selection and production collapses. The archive is no longer a system that chooses among a pre-existing body of statements, images, and events. It is a system that produces statements, images, and events — and then curates among its own outputs, in a closed loop that has no external referent.

The emerging model of agentic AI represents a further concentration of this archival power. Systems now being deployed by Google (Gemini as agent), Anthropic (Claude for computer use), and OpenAI (Operator) browse, summarize, and act on information autonomously on behalf of users. If the algorithmic feed already governs what the user sees, the AI agent governs what the user knows about what exists to be seen. The mediating layer becomes total: rather than filtering a world that the user could theoretically access by other means, the agent produces a world that exists only through its own outputs. This is #Derrida's archontic principle at its logical extreme.

AI Overview — Verify Source
In May 2024, Google rolled out AI Overviews — AI-generated summaries appearing above traditional search results for hundreds of millions of users globally. Within days of deployment, users documented the system advising them to add glue to pizza to prevent the cheese from sliding, to eat rocks for their nutritional value, and to smoke during pregnancy. An internal Google analysis of the Gemini model found the system produced incorrect information in approximately twenty-eight percent of cases — yet it was deployed to serve billions of searches. By February 2025, Google's AI Overview cited an April Fool's Day satirical piece about "microscopic bees powering computers" as factual information in live search results. A Harvard Kennedy School analysis published in 2025 characterized AI hallucinations as representing misinformation at a scale possibly unprecedented in the history of human civilization [Fig. 10]. These are not edge cases. They are the operational condition of a system in which the archive has begun to generate the documents it archives.

The design choice that makes this condition structurally dangerous is not the hallucination itself. It is the severing of the user from the source document. When Google's AI Overviews synthesizes an answer without linking to the underlying sources — a deliberate design choice — it does not merely curate what can be seen. It produces what appears to exist, on behalf of a corpus the user can no longer access. Foucault's archive governed what could be said. The generative feed governs what appears to have been said — and in the absence of the source document, the difference is invisible.

The stakes of this trajectory, understood through the cases above, are precise. #Noble documents how algorithmic oppression is fundamental to the operating system of the web. #Makhortykh et al. document how the visual archive of historical atrocity is being commercially rewritten by engagement metrics. #Hicks argues that the museum's violence is renewed daily in the act of display. If generative AI becomes the primary substrate of the feed, these patterns do not disappear. They accelerate — and they become structurally harder to audit, because the evidence of omission will no longer exist as a suppressed document that could in principle be recovered. It will simply never have been generated.

VI — Conclusion
Who Writes It Next

Return to the opening claim: every morning, before most people have had their coffee, an algorithm has already decided what kind of world they live in. This paper has argued that this decision is not neutral, not incidental, and not new. It is the latest instantiation of a power structure that has organized human knowledge — through archives, through museums, through editorial hierarchies — for as long as institutions have existed. What is new is the scale, the speed, the invisibility, and the imminence of a phase in which the archive no longer merely selects but creates.

But the deepest problem is not what the archive hides from you. It is what it prevents you from thinking. #Foucault argued that every historical period is governed by what he called an historical a priori — a set of conditions that determines not merely what is said, but what is thinkable at all. The archive does not merely censor; it constitutes the horizon within which thought becomes possible. What cannot be said cannot be thought. And what cannot be thought cannot be missed, questioned, or mourned. This is the move from epistemology to ontology: not you do not know this, but this does not exist as an object of your cognition.

You — the reader of this paper — are not outside this. You have arrived here through a series of information environments that have already shaped the edges of what strikes you as obvious, important, or worth knowing. The scholarship you have encountered, the news you have seen, the conversations that have felt possible: all of it has been pre-filtered by systems whose selection criteria you have never been shown and cannot audit from within. This paper does not exempt you from the archive. The most this analysis can do is name the structure — and insist that the structure exists.

The generative archive does not bury the card. It prints a new card, in real time, and places it in the sleeve. The reader pulls it out and reads it. It says something that was never written. And there is no original to compare it against, because the system that produced it has already displaced the system that would have stored the original. This is what Tetenbaum and Chen's Glimpse [2011] could not have anticipated. The book they made assumes that behind every window there is a card. The generative feed severs that anchor.

The stakes of the algorithmic archive were always, at their root, the stakes of thought itself: who gets to think what, under what conditions, on whose terms. The algorithmic archive is the first archival system to have successfully disguised itself as the world. And the generative archive is the first to have disguised itself as your own mind.

The question this paper leaves open is not whether the card will still be there to pull. The question is whether, in the archive that is coming, you will remember that you are pulling.
